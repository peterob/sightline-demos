Data Trust Architecture

Where does Sightline sit? What data can we trust? What's garbage-in/garbage-out?

The Fundamental Insight
The sell-side is trustworthy. The buy-side is not.

Shopify, Stripe, and banks have skin in the game—their business depends on accurate data. The borrower's assertions about POs, receipts, and inventory? They have incentive to lie.

Implication: We can build a high-trust repayment engine on Shopify data. The advance decision requires different verification—or different risk pricing.

The Architectural Implication
What We CAN Build with High Trust
  • Auto-repayment from Shopify sales (orders/paid → sweep)
  • Real-time revenue tracking per PO (SKU → PO mapping)
  • Sell-through velocity calculation (fulfillment events)
  • Refund/chargeback monitoring (disputes, refunds)
  • Cash position tracking (balance_transactions, payouts)
  • Projected payoff based on actual sales velocity
What Requires Additional Verification
  • Advance amount (depends on PO legitimacy, supplier confirmation)
  • Collateral value (depends on inventory actually existing)
  • Receipt confirmation (borrower-asserted, verify with carrier)
  • Inventory location (consider 3PL integration as trust anchor)
  • Cost basis (cross-reference with supplier invoice)

Why This Loop is High-Trust
Every step in this flow originates from a system with independent accountability:
  • Shopify — Their business depends on accurate order/payment data
  • Payment Processor — Regulated, audited, legally liable
  • Bank — Regulated, confirmed deposits are verifiable
  • Sightline — Deterministic computation on verified inputs

The borrower never touches this data. They can't fabricate sales, inflate revenue, or misreport payments. The repayment loop is self-enforcing.
The Advance Problem: Where Trust Breaks Down

The repayment loop is high-trust. But the advance decision depends on buy-side data that the borrower controls. This is where garbage-in/garbage-out risk lives.

The Advance Decision: What We Need to Verify
# To advance $40,000 against a PO, we need to trust: 1. PO is real └─ Borrower says they issued PO for $50K └─ Verify: Supplier confirmation? Payment to supplier? 2. Goods will ship └─ Supplier says they'll deliver 1,000 units └─ Verify: Deposit paid? Supplier track record? 3. Goods are received └─ Borrower says goods arrived at warehouse └─ Verify: Carrier delivery confirmation? 3PL receipt? 4. Goods will sell └─ Borrower believes product will sell at $99/unit └─ Verify: Historical velocity for similar SKUs? Market data? 5. Inventory exists └─ Borrower's WMS says 1,000 units on hand └─ Verify: 3PL confirmation? Periodic audit?
Option A: Trust Anchors

Add trusted third parties to verify buy-side events:

  • 3PL Integration — ShipBob, Deliverr confirm receipt/inventory
  • Supplier Confirmation — Supplier signs PO on Sightline
  • Payment Verification — Bank confirms wire to supplier
  • Carrier Tracking — Delivery confirmation from Maersk, FedEx
Option B: Risk Pricing

Accept lower trust, price it into the advance:

  • Lower Advance Rate — 60% instead of 80% until verified
  • Higher Fee — Charge for unverified risk
  • Escrow Structure — Hold advance until receipt confirmed
  • Track Record — Better terms as trust builds

The MVP Scope
Build the high-trust repayment engine first.

The Shopify integration gives us everything we need to:
  • Track sales per SKU in real-time
  • Map sales to POs (via SKU → PO mapping)
  • Compute waterfall allocation automatically
  • Verify cash received via balance_transactions
  • Execute sweep to lender on payout confirmation

The advance decision can start simple (manual underwriting, lower advance rates) and add trust anchors over time (3PL integration, supplier onboarding).

The repayment engine is the product. The advance process is risk management.
