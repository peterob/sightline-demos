01 · Why it happens

Four causes. Four effects. One missing primitive.

BEC is not a technology problem. It is a structural one. Every commercial payment today is authorized by one party. The vendor — the other party to the transaction — has no opportunity to confirm before money moves. The attack lives in that gap. Here is the chain.

Cause

Commercial payment authorization is unilateral.

One party approves the payment internally. The vendor learns of the transaction only when (or if) money arrives. Digital tools removed checks, branches, and paper, but left the trust model untouched.

Effect

The attacker only has to compromise one side.

A fraudulent bank-change request inserted into the payer's process has no counterparty challenge. The vendor isn't present in the authorization. The payment releases.

$3.04B in BEC losses in 2025. 86% of stolen funds moved via wire or ACH — inside real, working AP workflows, not at the perimeter. FBI IC3 2025 · 24,768 reported complaints · avg loss $122,977
Cause

Existing fraud tools validate the wrong thing.

Trustpair, Eftsure, Early Warning, and PaymentWorks all verify that bank account X is owned by vendor Y. They answer a question about identity, not authorization.

Effect

Both the real and the fraudulent account pass validation.

The attacker isn't using a fake account. They're using a real attacker's real account, claiming the vendor switched banks. Account validation says "the account is real" — and it is. The substitution survives the check.

BEC losses rose from $2.77B (2024) → $3.04B (2025) in a market where validation services are widely deployed. The validation layer is working. It is not the layer that closes this attack. FBI IC3 · YoY comparison
Cause

AP automation optimized for the wrong path.

Bill.com, Tipalti, AvidXchange, Stampli, HighRadius all chase touchless processing — straight-through invoice handling with no human review. Best-in-class teams reach 49%. The other 51% are exceptions.

Effect

The exceptions are exactly where fraud lives.

Bank changes, first payments, rush requests, threshold breaches — every high-risk payment is by definition an exception. Touchless automation routes those to the controller. It does not resolve them. The controller still verifies by phone, email, and Slack.

14% average exception rate, 9% best-in-class. 53% of AP teams rank exception handling as their single biggest operational challenge. Ardent Partners 2025 · Quadient 2025
Cause

The verification trail is a recollection, not evidence.

Phone-callback logs say "called and confirmed." Approval chains are Slack threads. Bank changes arrive as forwarded emails. These produce documentation, not verifiable documentation.

Effect

Audit becomes archaeology. Lender diligence becomes a coin flip.

At year-end, every sampled payment requires forensic reconstruction. The auditor asks "how did you know this bank change was legitimate?" and the answer is a screenshot. None of it is replayable. None of it would survive an adversarial review by a lender or insurer.

SOX-era controls require documentation. They do not require cryptographically verifiable documentation. This is the gap CAST closes.
02 · Why current tools miss

Each category solves a real problem. None of them solves this one.

The buyer asks "don't I already have tools for this?" The honest answer is yes — for adjacent problems. Here is each category, what it does well, and the specific reason it does not stop bank-change fraud.

Category 01

Bank account validation

Trustpair, Eftsure, Early Warning, PaymentWorks
What they do

Verify that the bank account a vendor claims to own is actually owned by that vendor. Use bank-network data, micro-deposits, or third-party validation services.

Why they miss

Validates account ownership at a moment in time. The attack happens between validations: a real vendor's real account exists, a real attacker's real account exists, the substitution happens at the moment of payment instruction. Both accounts validate cleanly.

What CAST adds

Confirmation of the payment intent, not the account identity. The vendor signs the exact amount and destination at the moment the payment is about to release.

Category 02

AP automation

Bill.com, Tipalti, AvidXchange, Stampli, HighRadius
What they do

Capture invoices, match to POs, route for approval, schedule payments. Reduce manual touchpoints in the non-exception path.

Why they miss

Optimized for the 49% of invoices that go touchless — which is not where fraud lives. Fraud lives in the 14% exception rate, where automation routes to a human with the same email-and-phone tools they had before.

What CAST adds

Gives the exception path a structural verification, not a human one. The controller is no longer the verifier — the vendor is.

Category 03

Email security

Abnormal, Proofpoint, Microsoft Defender
What they do

Detect malicious emails, phishing, and impersonation at the inbox layer. Block known attack patterns and anomalous senders.

Why they miss

BEC attacks increasingly originate from compromised legitimate accounts, not spoofed ones. The email is from the vendor — because the attacker is inside the vendor's email. Email security cannot tell the difference.

What CAST adds

Email is not the confirmation channel. Owning the vendor's inbox does not produce a passkey signature from the vendor's registered device.

Category 04

Manual SOX controls

Callback verification, dual approval, segregation of duties
What they do

Require a phone callback to a known vendor number, two-person approval, or separation between payment setup and release. Long-standing SOX-era discipline.

Why they miss

Produce documentation, not evidence. The callback log says "called and confirmed" — the recording, if it exists, is not bound to the specific payment. The dual-approval click is a workflow event, not a cryptographic proof.

What CAST adds

Produces the same kind of evidence a digitally-signed legal document produces — a binding signature over the exact terms, replayable by a third party using standard cryptography.

The differentiation, in one sentence
Other tools ask: is this account real? · did a human click approve? · was the email malicious?
CAST asks: did the vendor sign these exact terms with their own hardware?
That is the only question whose answer survives an adversarial reading by an auditor, a lender, or an insurer.
03 · How CAST works

One loop. Four steps. A signature that binds.

The architecture is simple. A trigger fires. A Trade Message goes to the vendor. The vendor signs the exact payment payload with a passkey. The payment releases — carrying a non-nullable foreign key to the signed event. If anyone changes one dollar of the amount or one digit of the account number after the signature, the signature no longer verifies.

04RELEASE

Payment releases

Auto-approve conditions evaluate against the policy version pinned on the bilateral event. Release authorized — carrying a non-nullable FK to the signed event.

writes release · lineage-bound
Why the binding matters The WebAuthn signature standard normally signs over a random challenge — proving "someone with this passkey was present at this moment." CAST replaces the random challenge with the canonical hash of the payment payload. The signature then proves something stronger: "the vendor approved these exact terms at this moment." Change a digit of the bank account, the amount, or the invoice number — the signature on the prior payload no longer verifies. This is the structural primitive every other claim on this page depends on.

04 · What we hold

Two trigger classes. One cannot be silenced by any admin, policy, or console.

Some triggers are policy decisions your controller tunes. Others are structural — written into the database schema, not configurable from any UI. The distinction is exactly what an audit committee asks about and what a CAST pilot proves.

Class A · Hard-coded

Schema-enforced. Non-suppressible.
Written into application code with no admin override path. No policy version can route around them. The Postgres role used by the app has no UPDATE privilege on these rules.
Bank account change on existing vendor always
First payment to a new vendor always
New bank account added < 30 days ago always
Why this matters: when your audit committee asks "what if someone disables the bank-change check?" — the answer is they can't. The check is in code, not configuration.

Class B · Policy-configurable

Tunable. Version-pinned at write time.
Your controller sets thresholds, exceptions, and routing per policy version. The version is pinned to every event — change rules going forward, prior events replay under the rules in force at the time.
Amount threshold breach per vendor / tier
Rush payment request dual approval
Duplicate invoice signature 90-day window
Off-cycle / unscheduled run per tenant
Why this matters: when policy changes, you don't have to retroactively reinterpret past payments. Each event carries the policy it was evaluated under.
05 · What we claim — and what we don't

We eliminate unilateral payment release for covered flows. We will not make a larger claim.

Every defensible architecture is precise about the line between what it closes and what it leaves to other controls. A claim that survives an adversarial reading by an auditor is the only claim worth printing on a buyer's whiteboard.

What v1 closes

For any covered payment, no release can occur without a counterparty-confirmed event signed by a passkey bound to a registered vendor device, with the WebAuthn challenge equal to the canonical payload hash of the payment.

What v1 does not close

Onboarding fraud. If a fraudulent vendor is registered with a real device, CAST will faithfully record their confirmations. Collusion. Two parties acting together to defraud a third can still do so — though the cryptographic record makes detection materially easier. Compromised devices. A stolen passkey under attacker control still signs valid events. These require upstream identity controls that CAST treats as preconditions, not replacements.

03 · ERRORS CAUGHT AT GATE

Payments where the vendor flagged a mismatch.

Wrong amount, wrong account, duplicate invoice. Every one is either a caught fraud or a caught data error — both valuable, both invisible today.

Expected: 1–3% of high-risk payments, on a clean AP function.
04 · BLOCKED-RELEASE COUNT

Payments where no bilateral event was produced, held at the gate.

Some will be legitimate non-responses. Some will be real signal — fraud attempted, vendor unreachable because the attacker has their email.

Each one is investigated. Each one produces an event-level audit trail.
05 · CONTROLLER HOURS RECOVERED

Measured against a baseline week before pilot start. The controller stops being the verifier of last resort.
