01 · The problem

Every commercial payment today is authorized unilaterally.

The payer approves an invoice internally. The vendor learns of the transaction only when — or if — money arrives. Digital tools removed checks, branches, and paper, but left the trust model untouched. A payment process where only one of two parties confirms terms before value moves is not bilateral authorization. It is a unilateral instruction with hope attached.

01

BEC works because of the model.

An attacker inserts a fraudulent bank change into a process that was never designed to verify the other party before payment. $2.9B in annual U.S. losses to a structural gap, not a sophistication gap.

02

The controller is the bottleneck.

Every exception — first payment, bank change, rush request, threshold breach — routes through a human checkpoint. Approval queues consume time the controller doesn't have, and approval is not verification.

03

Audit becomes archaeology.

At year-end, every sampled payment requires forensic reconstruction from emails, PDFs, and screenshots. The auditor does detective work because the payment record was never designed as an authorization chain.

Today

"The vendor sent us new bank details by email. We updated the file and paid."
  • One party approves; the other finds out at settlement.
  • Bank-change requests arrive as emails, not events.
  • Exceptions queue in the controller's inbox.
  • The "approval chain" lives in a Slack thread.

With CAST

"The bank change triggered a Trade Message. Riverdale confirmed on a passkey-bound link. The payment released against a sealed event."
  • Both parties co-author the authorization.
  • Hard-coded triggers cannot be silenced by policy.
  • Confirmations resolve in minutes, not hours.
  • Every release carries a tamper-evident proof.
02 · How it works

One party proposes. Both parties confirm. Then money moves.

The AP instantiation is one loop, four steps, no detours. The Trade Message is the only artifact the vendor ever sees. Everything else happens inside your controls.

Trigger fires

Your payment file lands. A trigger evaluates against the pinned policy — bank change, first payment, rush, threshold. A Proposed Event is written.

03 · What we hold

Two trigger classes. One cannot be silenced.

Some triggers are policy decisions your controller can tune. Others are structural — written into the schema, not configurable from any console. The distinction is what an audit committee asks about, and what a CAST pilot proves.

Class A · Hard-coded

Non-suppressible by policy.
Written into the database. No admin console can disable them. No policy version can route around them. They are structural invariants, not configuration.
Bank account change always · always · always
First payment to a new vendor always · always · always
New bank account on existing vendor always · always · always

Class B · Policy-configurable

Tunable per customer.
Your controller sets thresholds, exceptions, and routing per policy version. The version is pinned to every event — change rules going forward, prior events replay under the rules in force at the time.

04 · What we claim — and what we don't

We eliminate unilateral payment release for covered flows. That is the claim. We won't make a larger one.

Every defensible architecture is precise about the line between what it closes and what it leaves to other controls. A claim that survives an adversarial reading is the only claim worth printing on a buyer's whiteboard.

What v1 closes

For any covered payment, no release can occur without a counterparty-confirmed event signed by a passkey bound to a registered vendor device.

What v1 does not close

Onboarding fraud. If a fraudulent vendor is registered with a real device, CAST will faithfully record their confirmations. Collusion. Two parties acting together to defraud a third can still do so — though the record makes detection materially easier. Compromised devices. A stolen passkey under attacker control still signs valid events. These require upstream identity controls that CAST treats as preconditions, not replacements.
