Start here / What is CAST

A coordination protocol for proof.

CAST — Commercial Agreement Settlement Technology — is a bilateral coordination protocol for commercial payments and financial control. Before value moves, both parties co-author one cryptographically bound record of the transaction. That record becomes the authorization — and travels with the payment through the ERP, the audit, the lender, and the insurer as tamper-evident proof.

"

The proof is produced once, at transaction time. Verification cost drops to near zero, because checking a hash is structurally cheaper than reconstructing a decision chain.

The thesis

As AI commoditizes execution, the binding economic constraint shifts from production to verification. The question stops being can we produce this output and becomes can we prove this output is trustworthy. CAST expands the verifiable share of economic activity by replacing opinion-based reconstruction — an auditor reading email threads — with proof-based verification: a lineage-hash check anyone can run.

What CAST is not

  • Not an ERP replacement. It sits upstream as the authorization and proof layer, exporting posting-ready journals.
  • Not workflow automation. Workflows are consequences of events and policies — not the primary design object.
  • Not a fraud guarantee. CAST eliminates the class of fraud where one party acts without the other's knowledge. It makes collusion documented and detectable, not impossible.
  • Not a point solution. The AP product is the first instantiation of a vertical-agnostic architecture.

The verification gap
Start here / The verification gap

A unilateral instruction with a hope attached.

A payment process where only one of two parties has confirmed the terms before value transfers is not a bilateral authorization. The other party learns of the transaction only when money arrives, when it does not arrive, or when something is already wrong.

What the gap costs

FailureMechanism
Business Email Compromise
$2.9B annual US losses		A fraudulent instruction inserted into a process never designed to verify the counterparty before payment.
ReconciliationBoth parties keep independent records. When they disagree, both reconstruct from email threads and PDFs — slow, costly, inconclusive.
Audit frictionEvery sampled transaction requires forensic reconstruction. The auditor does archaeology, not verification.
Lender frictionField exams and advance-rate discounts exist because the underlying records are unilateral and cannot be trusted without independent checks.
"

Every downstream party — auditor, lender, insurer — must verify independently, expensively, and often inconclusively. CAST produces the proof once, so they don't have to.

The root cause is structural

This is not a software gap to be patched. The trust model underneath commercial payments was never bilateral. Card networks, ACH, wire — each absorbed change operationally without ever rebuilding the assumption that one party's instruction is sufficient to move value. CAST adds one narrow control: before a covered payment releases, the counterparty confirms the details through a channel cryptographically bound to a verified identity.

Why Confirm & Pay first
Start here / Why Confirm & Pay first

Why Confirm & Pay is our first product.

The architecture is vertical-agnostic. Confirm & Pay — the accounts-payable instantiation — is where we prove it. AP is not a niche; it is the cleanest place to demonstrate the bilateral event loop and capture the first 1,000 units of proof.

Four reasons AP goes first

  • The damage is concentrated. First payment to a new vendor, a bank-account change, a rush payment — a handful of triggers account for most unilateral-instruction fraud. We start exactly where the risk lives.
  • The loop is provable in one screen. One party proposes, the vendor confirms via passkey, policy governs release. That is the entire architectural claim, demonstrable end-to-end without GL automation downstream.
  • The vendor adopts nothing. The counterparty is a signer, not a tenant — a browser link, three buttons, no app, no account. Adoption friction is near zero.
  • Every other vertical is a configuration problem. GPU compute financing, institutional grants, intercompany transfers, trade finance — same four layers, different event types and policies. AP proves the architecture; the rest is seed data.
"

The investor evidence target is 1,000 bilateral events — not feature completeness. Build the loop first. Everything else comes after the first 50 events are live.

The covered controls

TRIGGER 01

First payment to a vendor

A new relationship should not receive funds without counterparty confirmation of the destination details.

TRIGGER 02

Bank-account change

Any destination change triggers confirmation. The vendor sees the bank last-four before release.

TRIGGER 03

Rush payment

Urgency increases scrutiny instead of bypassing controls.

TRIGGER 04

Amount threshold

Payments above a policy-set ceiling require the bilateral gate before they can post.

These triggers are hard-coded, not policy-configurable — by design. They sit at the boundary between measurable and judgment-dependent transactions. Making them configurable would create a suppression attack surface: a path by which a policy change, rather than a bilateral event, moves a high-judgment payment through without review. Hard-coding closes that path structurally.

The four layers
Architecture / The four layers

Four layers. Every vertical. No re-architecture.

Every commercial use case maps to the same four layers. The layers do not change between verticals — only the event types, policy rules, and Work Order structures do.

LAYER 01 · EVENTS

The only truth

Records everything that happens. All other layers are projections of this one. Immutable, append-only.

AP: Invoice ingested → Proposed. Vendor confirms → Bilateral. Payment settles → Settlement.
LAYER 02 · POLICIES

Versioned rule sets

Govern when an event needs a Work Order, what must complete before a Posting, and what happens on exception.

AP: Triggers — first payment, bank change, threshold. Seven auto-approve conditions, all must pass.
LAYER 03 · WORK ORDERS

The execution context

Where humans and agents do the accounting work between an event and a ledger entry. Three-track state.

AP: Analyst assigns GL code, checks budget, routes for approval — every decision recorded with actor identity.
LAYER 04 · LEDGER

Journal-ready output

Postings produced by completed Work Orders, carrying unbroken lineage from event to entry.

AP: Posting generated only after vendor confirms and controller approves. Non-nullable FK to the bilateral event.

Foundational design principles

PrincipleWhat it guarantees
Event sourcingThe event log is the system of record. Delete every other table and replay the log — they reconstruct exactly. The audit trail is the primary output, not a byproduct.
Deterministic state machinesThe same events produce identical outputs for all parties. Agreement is guaranteed by construction, not reconciliation.
Bilateral co-authorshipEvents are created jointly. Neither party can write to the shared log unilaterally.
SQL-level enforcementInvariants live in the schema, not the application. App code changes weekly; constraints survive every refactor and every future engineer.

The bilateral event loop
Architecture / The bilateral event loop

One party proposes. The vendor confirms. Policy governs release.

The loop is the entire architectural claim. Steps 1–5 are the prototype — sufficient to generate the proof and answer the three questions: do vendors confirm easily, do they confirm across companies, does audit effort decline.

01 · PROPOSE

Payment intent created

A high-risk invoice enters the queue. Policy version and payment details are snapshotted. Lineage hash computed.

02 · SEND

Trade Message sent

Vendor receives one browser link: amount, invoice, pay date, bank last-four, deadline.

03 · CONFIRM — the pivot

Vendor confirms via passkey

Passkey assertion verified, bound to the Work Order hash. The Bilateral Event is written. This is the moment proof is created.

04 · RELEASE

Payment can proceed

Covered payments stay blocked until the bilateral event exists. The proof record is retained and portable.

The five non-negotiables

Enforced in SQL, not application code. If you find yourself writing TypeScript to "check" one of these, the architectural claim is broken.

  • agent_id is non-nullable on every event — no anonymous writes.
  • Every posting has a foreign key to a bilateral event — no ledger entry without co-authored reality.
  • No posting reaches the ledger until the signature is verified — verification is a precondition to insert, not a status column.
  • Signatures are over canonicalized hashes, not rows — a 2026 signature still verifies in 2031 after the schema grows.
  • WebAuthn with required user verification — the signature is bound to a hardware-backed key, with stored attestation.

Compiled control plane
Architecture / Compiled control plane

Canonical truth below. Operable control above.

The control plane is a derived operational layer of refreshable projections over the canonical objects. It exposes the current proof, decision, and exception state of every Work Order — and it is replayable, never a source of truth.

The architecture stack

LayerContents and role
User / Agent surfaceQueue, detail, proof tasks, audit replay, governed actions.
Control planeControl Items, Invariant Results, Proof Requirements, Decision Views, History Views.
Canonical CAST layerEvents, Policy Versions, Work Orders, Postings, Cases, Vendors.

Actor attribution

"

Every event and every Work Order decision must declare whether the actor was human, agent, system, or counterparty — and must identify the accountable human owner whenever an action can change financial state.

This is the design rule that makes the AI Sandwich operable: a signature without context is a claim; a signature with a decision record is a proof.

Zero trust for money
Perspectives / Zero trust for money

Zero trust, applied to the movement of value.

Network security abandoned the trusted perimeter a decade ago — every request is authenticated, every action is verified, nothing is trusted by location. Commercial payments never made that shift. CAST is zero-trust architecture applied to money.

The implicit-trust perimeter still exists in finance

Most payment controls assume that an instruction originating inside the organization — from a known email, an approved user, a familiar vendor record — is trustworthy. That is the perimeter model. Business Email Compromise is simply an attacker crossing the perimeter once and inheriting all the trust inside it.

Zero trust replaces "trusted because of origin" with "verified at the point of action." CAST does exactly this for payments: no covered payment is trusted because of who submitted it. It is trusted only after the counterparty independently co-authors the terms through a cryptographically bound channel.

"

Never trust, always verify — extended from packets to payments. The bilateral gate is the verification step the payment perimeter never had.

Three zero-trust properties CAST inherits

  • Identity-bound action. WebAuthn ties each confirmation to a hardware-backed key — not a password, not an inbox an attacker can capture.
  • Least-privilege verification. The vendor sees only their own confirmation record. Buyer GL codes, budgets, and other vendors are never exposed across the counterparty surface.
  • Continuous verification. A bank-account change re-triggers the gate. Trust is not granted once and inherited forever — it is re-established at every covered event.

Validation & liability as a service
Perspectives / Validation & liability as a service

In the verified economy, value accrues to whoever can underwrite the output.

When AI commoditizes the production of financial output, generating a journal entry is no longer where value lives. Value moves to the party who can verifiably underwrite that the output reflects reality — and accept liability for it. Catalini, Hui & Wu call this Liability-as-a-Service.

The AI Sandwich

Research on AI economics identifies a durable topology for high-stakes professional work: human intent sets the policy and acceptance criteria, machine execution processes events and generates output, and human verification closes the loop with an approval that carries personal accountability. The Work Order is where the sandwich is made concrete.

TOP SLICE

Human intent

The controller defines the policy and the acceptance criteria — what a valid payment looks like.

FILLING

Machine execution

Agents ingest invoices, evaluate triggers, generate postings. Cheap, fast, commoditized.

BOTTOM SLICE

Human verification

A controller underwrites the output — staking professional judgment that the event is genuine.

THE PROOF

The decision record

Records the conditions evaluated, the budget position, and the approver's identity. The proof of underwriting.

"

A controller who approves a Work Order is not approving a payment. They are underwriting the output. That underwriting act is what makes a transaction insurable, financeable, and survivable.

Validation as a service

The bilateral proof is not just internal control — it is a product the downstream economy pays for. A third-party verification API lets an auditor, lender, or insurer confirm a specific bilateral event without contacting either party. Verification rents accrue to the layer that can produce inspectable proof on demand.

AI & agentic commerce
Perspectives / AI & agentic commerce

When agents transact, who co-authored the deal?

Autonomous agents will increasingly initiate payments, negotiate terms, and settle obligations. That makes the bilateral question more urgent, not less: when two agents transact, there must be a signed record both principals hold — and any third party can verify.

Attribution is the precondition

CAST already requires every event to declare its actor mode — human, agent, system, or counterparty — and to name the accountable human owner whenever an action can change financial state. This is exactly the control structure agentic commerce needs: an agent can execute, but a human remains accountable, and the record proves which was which.

"

An agent can be the performer. A human must remain the accountable owner. The decision record keeps the two distinct — permanently, and verifiably.

The agent loop, gated

  • Agents propose; the gate stays bilateral. An agent can draft and route a payment, but the covered event still requires counterparty co-authorship before value moves.
  • Policy as machine-checkable spec. Proposed policy changes can be expressed as formal state-machine specs, model-checked against all reachable states, and shadow-executed against history before any human approves them.
  • Proof travels with the transaction. Whatever produced the event — human or agent — the lineage hash and bilateral confirmation make it independently verifiable downstream.

The agentic future does not weaken the case for bilateral proof. It is the case for bilateral proof. As execution gets cheaper and faster, the only durable asset is the ability to prove, years later, that an event occurred and what it contained.
