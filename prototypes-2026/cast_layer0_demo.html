Internal Agent → Counterparty → Ledger Three scenes · One signed chain

Abilateral event is a fact two parties cryptographically agree to. This document walks the full loop — issue, confirm, post — as a demo customer will see it, annotated for the developers building it.

What the customer sees is a work order, a confirmation page, and a ledger entry — three ordinary screens. What the schema guarantees is that no ledger entry exists without a counterparty signature over the exact bytes of the work order. The cryptographic chain is the product; the screens are its packaging.

Each scene below corresponds to one endpoint and writes to specific tables. Non-negotiables 1–5 are enforced at the database layer, not by the screens.

01 Scene · Internal

A work order is issued — unsigned, awaiting the other side.

An internal operator drafts a purchase order against a registered counterparty. The payload is canonicalized and hashed the moment it is issued. Nothing posts yet.

02 Scene · Counterparty

The counterparty confirms — the signature binds to exact bytes.

The counterparty opens the confirmation URL on their own device. They review the terms, then sign with their registered authenticator. The WebAuthn challenge is the payload hash — meaning the signature cannot be replayed against any other payload.

03 Scene · Ledger

The ledger posts — and only now.

Once the signature row exists, and only then, the DB trigger permits a posting. Two ledger entries appear — a debit to inventory and a credit to the vendor payable — both foreign-keyed to the same signed event. The chain of evidence is complete.
