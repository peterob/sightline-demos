Margin Analysis · Dual Case

The Five Questions

A Case For · A Case Beyond

Every sign had to answer five questions before it made economic sense. Those questions contain the entire logic of margin analysis. Here we build the strongest case for that logic — then evolve it.

1 What does it cost to build & finance?
2 What revenue covers variable costs?
3 When does capital get recovered?
4 When does it contribute to fixed costs?
5 When does it generate owner profit?
The Case For

Why These Questions Are Exactly Right

Unit economics discipline prevents the most common cause of business failure: scaling a broken model. Complexity is the enemy of survival.

Q1 · Capital Clarity

You cannot manage what you cannot price

Most business failures trace to undercapitalization — not bad strategy. Forcing a precise cost-to-build answer at unit level prevents founders from hiding systemic capital problems inside blended averages. If you can't answer Q1 per unit, you don't understand your own business.

Q2 · Variable Cost Floor

Contribution margin is the minimum test of viability

A unit that doesn't cover its variable costs is actively destroying cash with every transaction. Volume cannot fix negative contribution margin — it accelerates failure. Q2 is the hard floor below which no strategic argument survives.

Q3 · Capital Recovery

Time is the one resource you cannot raise more of

Payback period forces honest dialogue about capital efficiency over time. A business that never explicitly asks "when does this come back?" is implicitly assuming infinite patience — which no capital source, owner, or market actually provides.

Q4 · Fixed Cost Absorption

Overhead is the silent killer of growing businesses

Fixed costs don't wait. Rent, payroll, and debt service accrue whether units perform or not. Sequencing each unit's contribution to fixed costs is the only way to know whether overhead is covered before scaling — the discipline that separates operators from optimists.

Q5 · Owner Profit

Profit is the score — everything else is commentary

Without an explicit timeline to owner profit, every other metric is a proxy. Q5 forces the discipline of connecting activity to reward — closing the loop between investment and return. Businesses that never ask this question tend to confuse revenue growth with business building.

The Evolved Framework

Five Better Questions for Complex Businesses

The original five are necessary but not sufficient. Each question contains a hidden assumption that breaks under network effects, portfolio logic, and strategic competition.

Replaces Q1

What is the unit's total system cost — including optionality value forgone?

Build cost is only one input. Capital allocated to this unit cannot be deployed elsewhere. The real question includes opportunity cost and strategic optionality: does this unit open markets, create barriers, or enable future units that the standalone calculation ignores?

Preserves: cost discipline · Adds: opportunity cost + strategic value
Replaces Q2

What is the unit's contribution including demand it generates for other units?

Variable cost coverage in isolation misses cross-subsidization and network spillovers. A loss-making unit that drives traffic to profitable units has a positive system contribution. The correct margin floor is portfolio-level, not unit-level.

Preserves: minimum viability test · Adds: system-level margin thinking
Replaces Q3

What is the risk-adjusted IRR, and how does it compare to alternatives?

Simple payback period treats a dollar in year 1 equal to a dollar in year 5, and ignores risk. IRR with scenario weighting — accounting for downside cases — gives a true picture of capital efficiency. A slower payback at higher certainty often beats a fast payback with high variance.

Preserves: capital recovery discipline · Adds: time value + risk adjustment
Replaces Q4

What fixed cost investment is required now to enable the unit economics of scale?

The original question treats fixed costs as given. But strategic fixed cost expansion — hiring ahead, building infrastructure, entering markets at a loss — is often what creates the unit economics that later justify the investment. The question should run both directions.

Preserves: overhead accountability · Adds: forward-looking infrastructure logic
Replaces Q5

When does this unit maximize enterprise value — and is that better than profit extraction?

Owner profit is one form of value realization. In competitive markets, reinvesting unit returns into growth often creates more owner wealth than extracting profit. The real question is whether profit should be taken now or compounded — a timing and reinvestment decision, not just a performance measure.

Preserves: return accountability · Adds: reinvestment vs. extraction logic
The Synthesis

When the originals win

Capital-constrained operators. Physical unit businesses. Franchise and licensing models. Environments where discipline prevents failure more than strategy creates upside. The original five are a survival tool — ruthlessly effective at their job.

When the evolved set wins

Platform and network businesses. Portfolio operators. High-growth competitive markets where speed and positioning matter more than current-period returns. Anywhere the unit is a means to a systemic end, not the end itself.
