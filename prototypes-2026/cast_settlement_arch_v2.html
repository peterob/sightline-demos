The three phases

One deal, one hash, three lifecycles.

Pre-settlement is where terms get pinned down. Settlement is the payment itself. Post-settlement is where auditors, lenders, and insurers consume the record. Pre-settlement produces a signed event. Settlement conditions a transfer on it. Post-settlement is the verifier surface — replay anywhere, no trust required. Pre-settlement is the wedge — Bitcoin can't reach it. Settlement is rail-agnostic. Post-settlement is where verification rents accrue and Layer 2 sells coverage.

Phase 01
Pre-settlement
Deal formation · bilateral agreement
The terms get nailed down before any money moves. Vendor confirms the exact PO with their device. Canonicalized payload, WebAuthn-bound signature. Posting refuses to exist without it. Highest-value layer. Real disputes originate here, where hidden constraints meet bilateral commitment.
Phase 02
Settlement
Atomic value transfer · on rail
Payment fires only because the agreed terms were satisfied. Reconciliation is automatic. Transfer conditioned on the verified event hash; rail produces immutable receipt. Atomicity binds off-chain agreement to on-chain fact. This is where chains earn their keep.
Phase 03
Post-settlement
Verification · audit · underwriting
Anyone — auditor, lender, insurer — can replay the deal years later. Verifier replays event chain via pinned canonicalizer. Standard libs, pass/fail. Verification rents. LaaS sits here. The signed bytes are the durable asset.

Why each phase matters

Three phases. Three audiences. One chain. Three phases. Three sets of primitives. One join key. Three phases. Three rent surfaces. One asset.

Phase 01 · Pre-settlement

The agreement gets locked in before money moves. Canonical bytes + WebAuthn — everything else rests on this. The wedge. Bitcoin and stablecoins don't reach here.

Procurement drafts a PO. The vendor confirms the exact terms with their device. Once that loop closes, no one can quietly alter the deal — and no entry hits the books without it.

This is what three-way matching has always wanted to be.

Signed object is sha256(payload_version || canonical_json(payload)). Challenge presented to authenticator is the payload hash — not a random nonce — so the signature binds permanently to bytes.

Schema enforces NN1–5: posting refuses to exist without a verified event signature.

Bitcoin and stablecoins are excellent at trace properties post-broadcast. They're weak at the bilateral commitment problem: hidden constraints, mutual acceptance, the moment of binding.

That's where real-world disputes originate. Cast occupies that ground.

Owner: Procurement, AP, vendor success. Tables: agents, webauthn_credentials, events, event_signatures, schema_registry. Defends Catalini's verification-rent thesis at the formation layer.
Phase 02 · Settlement

Payment fires automatically against the confirmed deal. Atomic binding — rail receipt references the event hash. Atomicity is what chains are good at. Here they earn it.

Treasury releases the payment. The transfer references the PO directly, so matching, posting, and reconciliation aren't a Friday-afternoon project — they happen at the moment the wire goes out.

The vendor's receipt closes their books in the same motion.

Settlement is conditional on the verified event. For stablecoin rails this is a hashlock or covenant; for traditional rails it's a treasury workflow that refuses to release without a valid event_id reference.

On-chain TX hash and off-chain event_id are mutually linked.

Settlement is rail-agnostic. Stablecoins, Lightning, ACH, wire — Cast doesn't compete with any of them. It composes with all of them.

The atomicity that crypto is good at is what binds the off-chain agreement to the on-chain receipt.

Owner: Treasury, AP. Reconciliation is automated. Rails: USDC, USDT, BTC L2, ACH, wire — interchangeable. Hybrid play: off-chain hyperproperty + on-chain atomicity.
Phase 03 · Post-settlement

Auditors, lenders, insurers all read the same record. Replay via pinned canonicalizer. Standard libs only. Verification rents. LaaS surface. Reinsurable pool.

Five years later, a bank asks for proof of a receivable. A regulator asks how an AP entry was authorized. An insurer asks who confirmed delivery.

The verifier replays the event chain and gets a yes/no — without phone calls, email archeology, or trust in any one party.

Verifier loads canonicalize@v1, recomputes bytes, checks ECDSA signature against public key, walks X.509 chain to manufacturer CA, validates on-chain TX.

Every primitive is a standard library. The moat is the schema, not a verifier binary.

This is where Catalini's rents live. Each verifier is paying for the ability to consume the signed record without trusting the originator.

The highest-premium policies cover the property itself — turning settlement into a full-stack insurable product.

Consumed by: auditors, lenders, insurers, regulators. Inputs: events, signatures, credentials, registry, postings, tx_hash. Layer 2 sells the replay. Layer 3 sells the policy.
