On phase 01 · Pre-settlement

The agreement gets locked in before the money moves. Canonical bytes + WebAuthn — the rest of the system rests on this. The wedge. Bitcoin and stablecoins don't help here.

Procurement drafts a PO. The vendor confirms the exact terms with their device. Once that loop closes, no one can quietly alter the deal — and no entry hits the books without it. This is what three-way matching has always wanted to be.

The signed object is sha256(payload_version || canonical_json(payload)). The challenge presented to the authenticator is the payload hash — not a random nonce — so the signature binds permanently to the bytes. Schema enforces NN1–5: agent_id non-null, posting refuses to exist without a verified event signature.

Bitcoin and stablecoins are excellent at trace properties post-broadcast. They are weak at the bilateral commitment problem: two parties' hidden constraints, mutual acceptance, and the moment of binding. That's where real-world disputes originate. Cast occupies that ground.

Owner: Procurement, AP, vendor success. Tables touched: agents, webauthn_credentials, events, event_signatures, schema_registry. Defends Catalini's verification-rent thesis at the formation layer.

On phase 02 · Settlement

Payment fires against the confirmed deal — automatically reconciled. Atomic binding: the rail's receipt references the event hash. Atomicity is what chains are good at. This is where they earn their keep.

Treasury releases the payment. The transfer references the PO directly, so the matching, posting, and reconciliation are not a Friday-afternoon project — they happened at the moment the wire went out. The vendor's receipt closes their books in the same motion.

Settlement is conditional on the verified event. For stablecoin rails this is a hashlock or covenant; for traditional rails it's a treasury workflow that refuses to release without a valid event_id reference. The on-chain TX hash and the off-chain event_id are both stored, mutually linked.

Settlement is rail-agnostic. Stablecoins, Bitcoin Lightning, ACH, wire — Cast doesn't compete with any of them. It composes with all of them. The atomicity that crypto is good at is precisely what binds the off-chain agreement to the on-chain receipt without re-litigating either.

Owner: Treasury, AP. Reconciliation is automated. Rails: USDC, USDT, BTC L2, ACH, wire — interchangeable. Hybrid play: off-chain hyperproperty + on-chain atomicity.

On phase 03 · Post-settlement

Auditors, lenders, insurers all read the same record — without trusting anyone. Replay via pinned canonicalizer — standard libs only, no proprietary verifier. Verification rents. LaaS premium surface. Reinsurable risk pool.

Five years later, a bank asks for proof of a receivable. A regulator asks how an AP entry was authorized. An insurer asks who confirmed delivery. The verifier replays the event chain and gets a yes/no — without phone calls, email archeology, or trust in any one party.

The verifier loads canonicalize@v1, recomputes the bytes, checks the ECDSA signature against the public key, walks the X.509 chain to the manufacturer CA, and validates the on-chain TX. Every primitive is a standard library; the moat is the schema, not a verifier binary.

This is where Catalini's rents live. Each verifier (auditor, lender, insurer) is paying for the ability to consume the signed record without trusting the originator. The highest-premium policies cover the property itself — "any execution that violates Cast's verified P1–P9 is covered" — turning settlement into a full-stack insurable product.

Consumed by: auditors, lenders, insurers, regulators. Inputs: events, event_signatures, webauthn_credentials, schema_registry, postings, tx_hash. Layer 2 sells the hosted replay; Layer 3 sells the policy.

Bottom line · operator Bottom line · engineer Bottom line · strategy

The PO, the GL entry, the payment, and the audit record all reference the same signed bytes. There is no moment where the four artifacts can drift apart. That is what makes the record defensible without anyone having to defend it.

Every phase produces a row that references event_id. payload_hash is the universal join key. The canonicalizer is committed code, versioned in schema_registry, so a 2031 verifier reproduces 2026 bytes exactly. No primitive used here is proprietary.

Cast occupies the layer Bitcoin and stablecoins can't reach: the bilateral commitment before broadcast. It then composes with any settlement rail and exposes the result to a verifier surface that compounds value with every event added. The wedge is pre-settlement; the moat is the cumulative artifact; the cash flow is post-settlement underwriting.
