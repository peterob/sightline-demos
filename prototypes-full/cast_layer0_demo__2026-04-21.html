Apurchase order is a promise, and a promise only counts if both sides agreed to the same terms. This walks one PO from issue, to vendor confirmation, to a posted accrual — the three screens your team lives in every day.

Abilateral event is a fact two parties cryptographically agree to. This document walks the full loop — issue, confirm, post — as a demo customer will see it, annotated for the developers building it.

What your team sees is a purchase order, a vendor confirmation page, and a ledger accrual — three ordinary documents. What the system guarantees is that no accrual hits the GL until the vendor has formally agreed to the exact terms on the PO. Three-way matching starts from a verified PO, not a guessed one.

Each scene corresponds to a step your procurement, AP, and finance teams already run. The difference: every step produces tamper-evident audit trail documentation that a lender, auditor, or board can rely on without calling anyone.

What the customer sees is a work order, a confirmation page, and a ledger entry — three ordinary screens. What the schema guarantees is that no ledger entry exists without a counterparty signature over the exact bytes of the work order. The cryptographic chain is the product; the screens are its packaging.

Each scene below corresponds to one endpoint and writes to specific tables. Non-negotiables 1–5 are enforced at the database layer, not by the screens.

01 Issue · Purchasing Scene · Internal

The PO is issued — authorized, awaiting vendor acceptance.

Your purchasing manager drafts the PO against an approved vendor. The system stamps the terms the moment they're authorized — no one can quietly edit the quantity or price after the fact. Nothing hits the GL until the vendor signs.

A work order is issued — unsigned, awaiting the other side.

An internal operator drafts a purchase order against a registered counterparty. The payload is canonicalized and hashed the moment it is issued. Nothing posts yet.

02 Accept · Vendor Scene · Counterparty

The vendor confirms — acceptance binds them to these exact terms.

The vendor's account manager opens the confirmation link on their own device, reviews the terms, and clicks to accept. Their acceptance is captured as a legally documented agreement — not an email thread, not a PDF signature, but a cryptographically sealed record tied to their identity and the exact PO contents.

The counterparty confirms — the signature binds to exact bytes.

The counterparty opens the confirmation URL on their own device. They review the terms, then sign with their registered authenticator. The WebAuthn challenge is the payload hash — meaning the signature cannot be replayed against any other payload.

03 Post · Finance Scene · Ledger

The accrual posts — and not one minute sooner.

Only after the vendor has formally accepted does the system post the accrual: a debit to raw-materials inventory and a credit to vendor payable. Every line in the GL is traceable back to a confirmed purchase order. When your auditor asks "what supports this accrual?", the answer is one click away.

The ledger posts — and only now.

Once the signature row exists, and only then, the DB trigger permits a posting. Two ledger entries appear — a debit to inventory and a credit to the vendor payable — both foreign-keyed to the same signed event. The chain of evidence is complete.
