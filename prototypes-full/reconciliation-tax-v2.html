Here's something software vendors won't tell you: the best possible outcome for their business is a problem that never fully goes away. Not maliciously. Just structurally. When you sell seats and subscriptions, you need ongoing usage. When you need ongoing usage, you need ongoing work. When you need ongoing work, you need problems that require continuous human effort to manage.

This creates a subtle but profound misalignment. The vendor's success depends on managing the problem. The customer's success depends on eliminating it.

For the past fifteen years, finance technology has optimized for the former while promising the latter. The result is a generation of tools that have made finance teams busier, not freer—and a profession bleeding talent because the work has become something people didn't sign up for.

The Shape of the Problem

Before we can understand why current solutions fail, we need to understand what finance teams actually do all day.

The textbook answer is analysis, forecasting, strategic partnership. The reality is different:

70–80% of finance work is reconciliation.

Not analysis. Not strategy. Matching. Checking. Verifying. Hunting for discrepancies between systems that should agree but don't.

The bank says one thing. The ERP says another. The CRM has a third version. Revenue recognition requires triangulating across all three plus the contract management system. Cash flow forecasting means rebuilding the same data from scratch because the AR aging report doesn't tie to the revenue schedule which doesn't tie to the collections log.

This is the actual job. And it exists because of a foundational architectural flaw: multiple systems recording overlapping events independently, with no shared source of truth.

Every "integration" between systems is just another reconciliation point. Every "consolidation" is just aggregating already-divergent data. The architecture guarantees the problem. The work is a symptom, not a root cause.

What Current Tools Actually Do

EPM/CPM Platforms

Anaplan, Adaptive, Vena, Pigment, Cube—they pull data from source systems into a modeling layer, provide better interfaces for building forecasts and budgets, and generate reports and dashboards.

BI and Analytics

Tableau, Power BI, Looker—they visualize data from source systems, enable self-service exploration, and create dashboards for stakeholders.

Workflow and Close Management

FloQast, BlackLine—they track the reconciliation process, manage the checklist of manual matching tasks, and document completion for audit.

Notice the pattern. Every tool accepts the reconciliation architecture as given and optimizes some aspect of living with it.

FloQast doesn't eliminate reconciliation—it makes tracking your reconciliation tasks more organized. Anaplan doesn't eliminate the need to pull and clean data—it gives you a better environment to work with already-cleaned data. Tableau doesn't eliminate the gap between systems—it helps you visualize whatever divergent data already exists.

These are tools for coping with the problem. Not solving it.

And they all require seats. Lots of seats. Administered by specialists. Creating new job functions that exist only because the tool exists.

The Seat-Based Trap

Consider the incentives more carefully. A vendor selling seats needs:

  • More users doing things in the platform—this justifies per-seat pricing
  • Specialized skills to operate the tool—this creates certified consultants, training programs, "Anaplan modelers" as a job category
  • Workflows that live inside the platform—the more you do there, the harder it is to leave
  • Ongoing work that requires the platform—problems that recur monthly, quarterly, annually

This is a perfectly rational business model. But notice what it optimizes for: sustained engagement with the platform, not resolution of the underlying problem.

If the reconciliation problem were actually solved—if systems shared truth rather than creating it independently—there would be less work. Less work means fewer seats. Fewer seats means less revenue.

So the market has evolved toward solutions that make the work more pleasant, more organized, more visible—but never less.

The work didn't disappear. It migrated. And now it's someone else's full-time job.

The Human Consequences

This isn't abstract. It's destroying the profession.

The work has become the wrong work

Young people enter finance expecting to analyze businesses, shape strategy, partner with operators. They find themselves instead running reconciliation checklists, chasing data discrepancies, formatting the same reports month after month. The interesting work—judgment, interpretation, decision support—occupies maybe 20% of their time. The rest is data janitorial work that exists only because systems were architected badly decades ago.

The skills have become non-transferable

When your value to an organization is knowing how to operate Anaplan, your career is tied to Anaplan's market position. You're not building financial judgment; you're building platform administration skills. This creates a perverse dynamic: the more "valuable" you become to your current organization (as the person who knows the tool), the less portable your skills are.

The talented people leave

The finance professionals with the most options—analytical, curious, ambitious—look at what the work actually entails and choose to do something else. Consulting. Tech. Startups. Anything but spending their careers as glorified data janitors.

CFO tenure has shortened

Despite better tools, more data, larger teams, CFO tenure has declined. The job has become impossible. You're responsible for insight and strategy, but your team spends 80% of its time on data plumbing. You're accountable for accurate forecasting, but your forecasts are built on reconciled data that's already stale when compiled.

The Root Cause No One Discusses

Here's what first-principles thinking reveals:

The problem isn't that reconciliation is hard. It's that reconciliation exists at all.

When multiple systems independently record overlapping events, reconciliation is mathematically guaranteed. You can make it faster, more automated, better tracked—but you cannot eliminate it without changing the architecture.

Every event in a business—an invoice issued, a payment received, inventory moved, a contract signed—is a single thing that happened. It has one truth. But current architecture records it multiple times in multiple places with no guarantee of consistency.

The bank has its version. The ERP has its version. The CRM has its version. None of them are wrong exactly—they're just partial views, recorded independently, with no shared reference point.

Reconciliation is the human (and increasingly automated) effort to make these partial views cohere. It's a tax we pay for architectural fragmentation. And no amount of platform sophistication eliminates it—because the platforms accept the fragmentation as given.

What First-Principles Architecture Looks Like

Imagine instead: a shared event log where each event is recorded once.

Not in your system and my system, reconciled later. In one place, cryptographically signed, with derived views for each party who needs to see it.

When a payment occurs, it's recorded once. The payer sees it. The receiver sees it. The bank sees it. There's no reconciliation because there's nothing to reconcile—everyone is looking at the same event.

When a contract is signed, its terms are encoded once. Revenue recognition, cash flow expectations, customer exposure—all derived from the same source. No triangulating between systems. No version conflicts. No monthly process to make things match.

Old Model

Multiple systems of record → manual reconciliation → unified reports → static presentations

New Model

Shared event log → derived views for each stakeholder → automatic explanation of changes

In the old model, humans are the integration layer. Their job is to make disconnected systems cohere. Every platform that "helps" them is actually just giving them better tools for a job that shouldn't exist.

In the new model, the log is the integration layer. It's mathematically complete—every event is recorded once, cryptographically signed, and visible to all parties who need it. The finance team's job shifts from data gathering to interpretation. The board sees the same underlying truth management sees, just filtered to what's relevant for governance.

The Choice

Every finance leader faces a decision, whether they recognize it or not:

Option A: Optimize the current architecture

Buy better tools for reconciliation. Hire more specialists to operate them. Accept that the work is what it is. Watch talented people leave. Compress ever-larger demands into ever-shorter tenures.

Option B: Change the architecture

Adopt systems where events are recorded once and shared. Eliminate reconciliation rather than managing it. Free finance teams to do finance. Give boards truth in real time.

Option A is the default. It requires no new thinking—just more of the same, optimized slightly.

Option B is harder. It requires admitting that fifteen years of finance technology investment has been solving the wrong problem. It requires building something different, not buying something incremental.

But Option B is also the only path to a profession that talented people want to join—and a function that actually delivers on its promise of strategic partnership.

The reconciliation tax has been collected long enough. It's time to stop managing it and start eliminating it.
