The Profit Engine

Four layers of margin intelligence — from scoreboard to source. Diagnose downward, fix at the root.

1
Financial Outcome
Gross Profit Margin
The scoreboard
"It tells you the engine changed. It does not tell you where or why."
This is a lagging indicator — the final output of dozens of upstream decisions. Optimizing here without understanding the source risks fixing the number while leaving the actual problem untouched. Margin moved. That's the only fact Layer 1 gives you.
↓ Localize the drift
2
Operational Outcome
Contribution Profit by Segment, Product & Channel
Where did the margin actually change?
"A blended 200bp decline might reflect a 600bp collapse in one segment offset by improvement elsewhere."
You cannot see this from Layer 1 alone. The mix effect compounds: segment composition can shift in ways that destroy blended margin even when no individual segment has worsened. This layer decomposes the signal into addressable geography.
Segment margin Product mix Channel economics Mix-shift effect
↓ Detect early signals
3
Operational Signals
Unit-Rate Signals by Function
Leading indicators — visible weeks before the P&L
"A pipeline weighted toward lower-margin segments today predicts a mix variance problem 6–8 weeks from now."
Cost-side signals — delivery cost per customer, infrastructure cost per unit, onboarding cost per new customer, support cost per customer — move first. Pair with sales engine signals: pipeline coverage ratio, stage conversion rate by segment, and revenue per rep. Together, they forecast both rate and mix drift before it reaches the scoreboard.
Delivery cost / customer Infra cost / unit Onboarding cost / customer Pipeline coverage ratio Conversion by segment Revenue / rep
↓ Name the root cause
4
Root Cause
The Decision, Policy, or Contract Term
Not a category — a decision that can be named, owned, and changed
"Each has a date, an owner, and a downstream cost consequence that was either not calculated or accepted without visibility into its compounding effect."
The three most common sources: deal structure drift (pricing concessions that compound), segment composition creep (gradual shift toward lower-margin customers), and process policy changes (operational decisions with untracked cost consequences). Every margin problem traces back to a specific decision someone made on a specific date.
Deal structure drift Segment composition creep Process policy changes

Read upward to diagnose. Read downward to fix.
The P&L tells you something changed. Only Layer 4 tells you what to do about it.
