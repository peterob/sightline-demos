The Coordination Problem

Why multi-party financial operations are broken, and how event-sourced architecture fixes them.

The Problem We Solve

Every business that involves more than one party—a lender and a borrower, a marketplace and its sellers, a franchisor and its franchisees—faces the same fundamental problem: they don't share a source of truth.

Each party maintains their own system of record. They export data, send it to counterparties, and hope it matches. When it doesn't—and it always doesn't—they reconcile. They argue. They adjust. They write off. They hire more accountants.

The reconciliation problem isn't a bug. It's the architecture.

When two parties maintain separate systems, disagreement is inevitable. Reconciliation is just the process of discovering how much you disagree and negotiating a settlement.

This isn't a minor operational annoyance. It's a fundamental constraint on what kinds of financial products are economically viable:

  • High-frequency, low-value transactions become uneconomical because reconciliation cost exceeds transaction value
  • Multi-party arrangements become exponentially complex as each party adds their own system
  • Real-time visibility is impossible because "truth" doesn't exist until reconciliation completes
  • Automation stalls because you can't automate decisions on data you don't trust
WHY NOW

Why Current Solutions Are Not Adequate

ERP Systems: Single-Party by Design

NetSuite, SAP, QuickBooks—these are excellent systems for managing a single company's operations. But they are architecturally incapable of solving multi-party coordination because they are designed around a single tenant's view of the world.

When a lender needs to see a borrower's inventory, they can't query the borrower's ERP directly. They receive a report—a static snapshot that's stale before it arrives and unverifiable by construction.

APIs and Integrations: Moving Data, Not Truth

Modern systems are better connected than ever. Shopify has APIs. Banks have APIs. ERPs have APIs. But APIs move data, not shared understanding.

When you pull data from Shopify into your system, you now have a copy. That copy can drift. It can be misinterpreted. The rules you apply to it may differ from the rules Shopify applies. You've moved information, but you haven't established agreement.

Reconciliation Software: Optimizing the Wrong Thing

Tools like Trintech, BlackLine, and FloQast are genuinely useful. They make reconciliation faster. But they accept the premise that reconciliation is necessary in the first place.

Reconciliation software is like building a faster ambulance instead of preventing the accident. It treats the symptom (disagreement) rather than the cause (separate systems of record).

Blockchain: Right Intuition, Wrong Abstraction

Distributed ledger technology correctly identified the problem: multiple parties need to agree on a shared state without trusting a central authority. But the implementation is wrong for most financial operations:

  • Consensus mechanisms optimize for adversarial environments, not business partnerships
  • Smart contracts are inflexible—real business logic needs versioning and amendment
  • Privacy and permissioning are afterthoughts
  • The "code is law" philosophy ignores that disputes are inevitable and require resolution
THE ARCHITECTURE

The Sightline Approach

We start from a different premise: if all parties compute the same function on the same events, they will arrive at the same state.

This is not a philosophical statement. It's an architectural one. Instead of each party maintaining their own system and reconciling afterward, we create a shared computation layer where:

  1. Events are captured from high-trust sources—systems with independent accountability like Shopify, 3PLs, payment processors, and banks
  2. Policy is versioned and deterministic—the rules for interpreting events are explicit, immutable, and pinned to each transaction
  3. State is derived, not stored—current positions are computed from the event stream, so any party can verify by replaying
  4. Actions are triggered by computed state—when conditions are met, execution is automatic

The key insight is that reconciliation becomes unnecessary when all parties are running the same computation. There's nothing to reconcile because there's no divergence.

The Primitives

We've identified seven primitives that are necessary and sufficient for modeling any governed financial operation:

Primitive What It Answers
Event What happened? An immutable fact from a trusted source.
PolicyVersion How do we interpret it? Versioned, deterministic rules pinned to each transaction.
Obligation What is owed? A commitment between parties with a defined lifecycle.
Allocation How is it decomposed? Line items, splits, and dimensional tagging.
Settlement How was it fulfilled? Partial or complete satisfaction of an obligation.
Posting How is it recorded? Double-entry ledger representation.
Case What needs resolution? Exceptions, disputes, and holds requiring human judgment.

Everything else—invoices, purchase orders, loans, payouts, subscriptions—is a projection over these primitives, not a new object. This is what makes the system universal: the primitives don't change when you move from inventory financing to marketplace payouts to grant management.

The Role of Data Trust

Shared computation only works if you can trust the inputs. We explicitly classify every data source by trustworthiness:

Trust Level Criteria Action
High Source has independent accountability (their business depends on accuracy) Automate
Medium Source has aligned but not identical incentives Verify
Low Source has incentive to misrepresent Require independent verification

This isn't just a classification exercise. It determines what can be automated and what requires human judgment. High-trust data flows directly into computation. Low-trust data either gets verified against a high-trust source or gets priced as risk.

The 3PL as trust anchor: When inventory ships to a third-party logistics provider and customer orders fulfill from that 3PL, the borrower never touches the goods. Receipt, storage, count, and shipment are all verified by an independent party with contractual liability. This converts "borrower-reported inventory" (low trust) into "3PL-verified inventory" (high trust).

What This Enables

Products That Weren't Economically Viable

PO-level inventory financing—where each purchase order is financed as an isolated silo with automatic repayment from sales—has always been theoretically possible. But the reconciliation cost made it uneconomical. When you can trust the sales data and automate the waterfall, the unit economics work.

Real-Time Visibility Without Reconciliation Lag

A lender financing inventory doesn't have to wait for a monthly borrowing base certificate. They see every sale as it happens, verified by Shopify. They see every inventory movement as it happens, verified by the 3PL. The "current position" is always current because it's computed, not reported.

Risk Isolation at Transaction Level

When each PO is a separate financing silo, a slow-moving SKU doesn't contaminate the rest of the portfolio. The lender's exposure is granular. The borrower's performing inventory isn't held hostage by one bad product decision.

Audit as Replay

When state is derived from events + policy, audit becomes trivial: replay the computation. The auditor doesn't have to trust your books—they can verify by running the same function on the same events.

CONCLUSION

The Bet We're Making

The financial infrastructure built over the last 50 years assumed that parties would maintain separate systems and reconcile periodically. This made sense when computation was expensive and communication was slow.

Neither of those constraints holds anymore. Computation is cheap. Data flows in real time. The only thing missing is an architecture that takes advantage of these facts.

Sightline is that architecture. We're not building better reconciliation software. We're building infrastructure that makes reconciliation unnecessary.

"When all parties compute the same function on the same events, there is nothing to reconcile."

The first application is PO-level inventory financing—a use case that demonstrates the architecture on a real problem with quantifiable value. But the primitives are general. The same infrastructure that powers inventory financing can power marketplace payouts, franchise royalty collection, grant management, or any other multi-party financial operation.

The goal is not to build a product. It's to build the coordination layer that future products compile down to.
